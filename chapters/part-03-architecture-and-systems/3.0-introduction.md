# 3.0 Introduction to Part 3: Architecture and Systems

Architecture is the set of decisions that are expensive to reverse: how a system is decomposed, how its parts communicate, how its data is modeled, and what properties the whole must exhibit under load and under failure. Part 3 is about making those decisions deliberately. For a small team, architecture can live in a few heads and evolve informally. For a large organization — hundreds of engineers, dozens of teams, systems that will outlive the careers of the people who build them — architecture becomes the coordination substrate. When it is clear, teams move independently without colliding. When it is vague, every cross-team dependency becomes a negotiation and every incident becomes an archaeology project.

These stakes are highest in enterprise and government. A tax engine, a benefits platform, a national health record, or a bank's core ledger is long-lived, heavily regulated, shared across departments, and subject to public accountability. Decisions made today about coupling, data ownership, and quality attributes constrain what is possible for a decade or more. Regulators and auditors increasingly expect documented, defensible architecture — evidence that reliability, security, privacy, and durability were designed in rather than bolted on. The failures that make headlines are architectural failures: portals that buckle on launch day, filing systems that time out at the deadline, migrations that lose or double-count records.

This part moves from the durable fundamentals through concrete structural choices, into the realities of distribution, data, and scale, and finally to the hardest problem most large organizations actually face — modernizing the systems they already run. The through-line is that good architecture is a series of conscious trade-offs, not a fashionable default.

## Chapters in this part

- **3.1 Architecture fundamentals** — The durable tools that survive technology fashion: quality attributes (the "-ilities"), architecturally significant requirements, fitness functions and evolutionary architecture, lightweight documentation with C4 and arc42, and structured trade-off analysis.
- **3.2 Architectural styles and patterns** — A survey of the major system shapes — monolith through microservices, event-driven architectures with CQRS and event sourcing, service mesh and gateways, serverless, and hexagonal and clean architecture — with guidance on when each fits, framed by Conway's Law.
- **3.3 Distributed systems** — The hard truths that appear the moment you cross a network boundary — unreliable networks, partial failure, no shared clock — and the standard defenses: consistency reasoning, idempotency, retries with backoff, circuit breakers, sagas, and distributed observability.
- **3.4 Data architecture and storage** — How data is modeled, stored, kept consistent, and served fast at scale: the major storage paradigms and when to use each, polyglot persistence, schema evolution and migration, caching and CDNs, and transactions and concurrency under load.
- **3.5 Scalability, performance, and resilience** — Three distinct qualities designed in rather than retrofitted: horizontal and vertical scaling, statelessness and sharding, load balancing and autoscaling, performance budgets, resilience patterns and chaos engineering, and multi-region disaster recovery framed by RTO and RPO.
- **3.6 Legacy modernization** — The incremental patterns that actually work — strangler fig and branch-by-abstraction — plus legacy risk assessment, mainframe and COBOL stewardship, data migration and dual-running, and how to resist the big-rewrite temptation that produces the field's most expensive failures.

## How these chapters interrelate

The chapters build on one another in sequence. Chapter 3.1 supplies the vocabulary — quality attributes and trade-off analysis — that every later chapter applies; the "-ilities" it names are precisely what chapters 3.4 and 3.5 make concrete. Chapter 3.2 turns those fundamentals into structural choices, and the more distributed styles it endorses (microservices, event-driven, service mesh) immediately incur the costs that chapter 3.3 teaches you to manage. Chapters 3.3, 3.4, and 3.5 are deeply interdependent: distribution forces the consistency and durability decisions of data architecture, and both distribution and data shape what scalability and resilience are achievable. Chapter 3.6 closes the loop by acknowledging that most large organizations are not building on a blank page — they are evolving systems of record that constrain every choice the earlier chapters describe.

Part 3 also connects outward. Chapter 3.2 depends on the design principles and Domain-Driven Design of chapter 2.2 to draw good service boundaries. The operational disciplines that keep these systems running belong to Part 9 — site reliability engineering (chapter 9.1) and observability (chapter 9.2) are where architectural resilience is proven in production. The security and privacy properties that regulators demand are designed here but detailed in Part 6, and the platform and delivery practices in Part 8 determine whether an architecture can actually be shipped and operated by many teams at once.
