# 10.0 Introduction to Part 10: Program Delivery, Risk, and the Enterprise/Government Context

Most of a guidebook like this teaches how to build software well. This part is about everything that surrounds the building when the organization doing it is large, long-lived, and answerable to others. A single team can succeed on informal alignment, implicit risk judgment, and a codebase it rewrites at will. An enterprise or government agency running dozens or hundreds of teams cannot: work competes for scarce budget and shared platforms, failure has consequences measured in money, safety, or public trust, and the organization must prove to regulators, auditors, and citizens that it is doing the right thing — not merely assert it. Part 10 covers the disciplines that turn strategy into shipped, sustainable, trustworthy software at that scale.

These topics matter especially in enterprise and government because the constraints are harder and the accountability is external. Annual appropriations and multi-year capital funding shape what can be built and when. Procurement law governs how components and vendors enter. Systems must outlive the people who built them and the companies that produced them, because a government cannot stop paying pensions while it refactors. And because many public systems are not optional — a citizen cannot choose a competitor for their tax authority — the obligation to be fair, transparent, and accountable is not a nicety but a duty. What follows treats these as engineering leadership concerns, not paperwork to delegate.

The through-purpose of the part is stewardship at scale: deciding what to build and funding it wisely, understanding and proving control over what could go wrong, consuming and contributing software deliberately, sustaining critical systems across decades, and serving people with dignity throughout. Done well, none of this is bureaucratic overhead; it is how a large organization earns and keeps the right to operate.

## Chapters in this part

- **10.1 Portfolio and program management** — Decide what a large organization should build, fund and sequence it across many teams, and steer toward strategic outcomes rather than isolated outputs, managing dependencies, vendors, and multi-year funding cycles as first-class concerns.
- **10.2 Risk, audit, and assurance** — Understand what could go wrong across systems, suppliers, and data, decide what to do about it, and produce evidence that the controls you claim actually work — bringing enterprise risk discipline to software without smothering delivery.
- **10.3 Procurement, open source, and licensing** — Consume and contribute software deliberately, with a strategy, license compliance, an understanding of copyleft obligations, and a plan for end-of-life, while meeting government mandates to prefer and publish open source under "public money, public code."
- **10.4 Sustaining large and long-lived systems** — Keep old, large, critical systems reliable, secure, understood, and changeable across decades and generations of staff, through ownership continuity, bus-factor mitigation, knowledge transfer, and deliberate deprecation.
- **10.5 Ethics, accountability, and public interest** — Take responsibility for what software does to people when it makes consequential decisions, treating accessibility, equity, transparency, fairness, and dignity as engineering requirements rather than features bolted on at the end.

## How these chapters interrelate

The through line runs from choosing the work to answering for it. Portfolio and program management (10.1) sets what gets funded and built; risk, audit, and assurance (10.2) governs the exposure that portfolio creates, and the two meet directly at vendor governance and supply-chain risk, where a portfolio decision to buy or outsource becomes a risk the organization inherits. Procurement, open source, and licensing (10.3) is where much of that supply chain actually enters, so its inventory and license discipline feed the risk registers and software bills of materials of 10.2. Sustaining long-lived systems (10.4) is what the portfolio commits to for decades once the building is done, and its concerns — concentration of knowledge, end-of-life, ownership continuity — are risk topics viewed across a long horizon. Ethics, accountability, and public interest (10.5) sits over all of it: the reason the whole apparatus of funding, control, and stewardship exists is to serve people well and account for the consequences.

The part also connects outward. Supply-chain integrity, SBOMs, and provenance in 10.2 and 10.3 build on the security and compliance practices in chapter 4.6, and license scanning belongs in the same delivery pipelines described in Part 8. Sustaining systems in 10.4 draws on the reliability, ownership, and operational-knowledge disciplines of Part 9. Portfolio management in 10.1 shares its roots with the delivery and leadership topics of Part 11 and the project-management practices of Part 12, such as chapter 12.1, while its outcome focus echoes the measurement culture of chapter 7.5. Read together, these chapters describe how a large organization converts strategy into software that is worth building, safe to run, sound to depend on, and fit to be trusted.
