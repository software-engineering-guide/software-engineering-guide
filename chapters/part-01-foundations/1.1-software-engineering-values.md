# 1.1 Software engineering values

## Overview and motivation

Software engineering values are the set of shared beliefs, norms, and everyday behaviors that shape how people build software together. It is not the posters on the wall or the values printed in the employee handbook; it is what actually happens when an incident wakes someone at 3 a.m., or when a junior engineer disagrees with a principal engineer, or when a deadline collides with quality. Values are the invisible system beneath every technical decision. 

In a small team, values propagate by osmosis: people sit together, absorb norms, and self-correct. In a larger team, osmosis fails, and values must be made explicit, written down, modeled by leaders, and reinforced through systems, or it fragments into dozens of incompatible micro-cultures that quietly tax every collaboration.

For a larger team, the stakes are structural. Weak values shows up as attrition, slow decision-making, hoarded knowledge, and repeated incidents whose root causes are never fully addressed. Strong values show up as fast, safe, reliable changes: software engineers surface problems early, learn from failure, and take ownership. The difference between these two value states is often larger than any technology choice.

Enterprises and government organizations feel this acutely because they operate at scale, under scrutiny, and during long timelines. Systems built today may run for a decade or more, staffed by people who have never met the original authors. In these environments, culture is the mechanism that transmits intent across time and turnover.

Regulated enterprises face an additional pressure: the temptation to substitute process for trust. When accountability is high and mistakes are visible, organizations often respond by adding controls, sign-offs, and blame. This is understandable but counterproductive. The most reliable, secure, and compliant organizations are usually those with the strongest learning cultures, not the most punitive ones. Values and compliance are allies, not opposites.

## Key principles

- Psychological safety is the foundation; without it, every other practice degrades.
- Failure is data. Blameless learning turns incidents into durable improvement.
- Ownership means accountability for outcomes, not just outputs: "you build it, you run it."
- Writing is thinking. A culture that writes decisions down scales its judgment.
- Sustainable pace beats heroics; burnout is a systems failure, not a personal one.
- Diversity, equity, and inclusion are engineering strengths that improve decision quality.
- Values are modeled top-down and reinforced bottom-up; leaders' actions outweigh their words.

## Recommendations

### Build psychological safety deliberately

Psychological safety is the shared belief that one can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and challenge decisions without fear of humiliation or punishment. It is the single strongest predictor of team effectiveness in large-scale studies. Build it by having leaders model fallibility ("here is a mistake I made and what I learned"), by responding to bad news with curiosity rather than punishment, and by explicitly inviting dissent in meetings. Rotate who speaks first so senior voices do not anchor discussion. Make it normal to say "I don't know" and "I need help."

### Practice blameless learning

When something breaks, focus on the conditions that allowed the failure, not the individual who triggered it. Adopt blameless postmortems: a written record of what happened, the timeline, contributing factors, and concrete action items with owners and dates. The guiding assumption is that everyone acted reasonably given the information they had. Ask "what made this easy to get wrong?" rather than "who messed up?" Track action items to completion; a postmortem culture that never closes its follow-ups becomes theater.

### Establish clear ownership models

"You build it, you run it" makes the team that writes a service responsible for operating it, including on-call. This tightens the feedback loop between design decisions and operational pain, which improves quality. Pair it with a service catalog that records, for every system, who owns it, how to reach them, its dependencies, and its runbooks. Ownership must be explicit and non-overlapping; ambiguous ownership is how systems rot and incidents linger. Where a team cannot realistically operate a system, provide platform support rather than diffusing accountability.

### Cultivate a writing culture

Writing improves clarity and creates artifacts that scale across time zones and years. Institutionalize design docs and decision records for significant changes: a short document stating the problem, options considered, the proposed approach, and the trade-offs, circulated for comment before implementation. This surfaces disagreement early, when it is cheap, and creates a durable record of why decisions were made. Keep templates lightweight and expectations proportional to the decision's weight. Reward good writing publicly.

### Protect sustainable pace

Hero culture, where a few individuals repeatedly rescue the organization through unsustainable effort, is a symptom of systemic weakness, not a virtue. It burns people out, concentrates knowledge dangerously, and masks the underlying problems that should be fixed. Measure and manage on-call load; if one person is paged constantly, treat that as a defect to engineer away. Normalize taking time off, respect focus time, and judge output over a quarter rather than a week.

### Treat DEI as an engineering strength

Diverse teams make better decisions because they consider more perspectives and are less prone to groupthink and blind spots, which matters enormously for accessibility, security, and serving broad populations. Build inclusion into everyday engineering: accessible documentation, inclusive language in code and interfaces, meeting practices that let quieter voices contribute, and equitable distribution of glamour work and glue work.

## Trade-offs: pros and cons

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Blameless postmortems | Surfaces real root causes; builds trust; drives systemic fixes | Can feel like "no accountability" to outsiders; requires discipline to close actions |
| "You build it, you run it" | Tight quality feedback loop; clear ownership | On-call burden; needs strong platform support to avoid burnout |
| Docs-first / RFC culture | Durable decisions; scales across turnover; async-friendly | Slower for trivial changes; risks bureaucracy if over-applied |
| Sustainable pace | Retention, reliability, long-term velocity | Feels slower during crunch; requires leadership to hold the line |

The central tension is between short-term speed and long-term health. Heroics and blame produce a burst of apparent control and then a slow collapse in morale and reliability. Blameless learning, ownership, and sustainable pace feel slower in any single week but compound into far higher velocity over quarters and years. Leaders must be willing to absorb short-term discomfort to protect long-term capacity.

## Examples

Enterprise example: A large financial-services firm suffered a major outage when a routine configuration change cascaded across services. In a blame culture, the engineer who pushed the change would have been reprimanded and the story would end there. Instead, a blameless postmortem revealed that the deployment tooling made the dangerous change indistinguishable from a safe one, that no staged rollout existed, and that the runbook was outdated. The firm invested in progressive rollouts and configuration validation. Similar changes now fail safely. The values choice to look at the system rather than the person produced a durable engineering improvement.

Government example: A government digital service agency adopted "you build it, you run it" alongside a strict docs-first RFC process. Because systems must survive changes in political administration and staff turnover measured in years, every significant decision is recorded in a design doc explaining the context and trade-offs. New engineers, and incoming contractors, can read the reasoning behind a decade-old architecture rather than reverse-engineering it. This written institutional memory is what allows the agency to maintain reliable public services despite high personnel turnover and strict accountability requirements.

## Business case: motivations, ROI, and TCO

The return on culture is real but indirect, which is why it is chronically underfunded. The dominant cost of software over its lifetime is not initial construction; it is maintenance, incident response, rework, and the cost of losing and re-hiring skilled people. A strong learning culture improves all of these areas. Blameless postmortems reduce repeat incidents. Ownership models reduce mean time to recovery. Writing culture reduces the cost of onboarding and the cost of decisions made in ignorance of prior reasoning.

Consider attrition alone. Replacing a mid-level engineer typically costs somewhere between half and two times their annual salary once recruiting, ramp-up time, and lost institutional knowledge are counted. If a healthier culture reduces regretted attrition by even a few percentage points across a thousand-person organization, the savings dwarf the modest cost of running postmortems and writing docs. The adoption cost is mostly leadership attention and a small amount of process overhead. The cost of not adopting is paid continuously and invisibly: slower delivery, recurring incidents, and quiet talent flight.

To make the case to leadership, connect culture to metrics executives already track: delivery lead time, change failure rate, mean time to recovery, incident recurrence, and regretted attrition. Frame psychological safety not as a soft benefit but as the mechanism that makes every other engineering investment pay off, because unsafe teams hide the very problems those investments are meant to fix.

## Anti-patterns and pitfalls

- Blame-and-shame incident reviews — drive problems underground; people stop reporting.
- Hero worship — rewarding firefighting over fire prevention perpetuates the fires.
- "Values" that leaders violate — stated values contradicted by behavior breed cynicism.
- Ownership without support — assigning on-call for systems teams cannot realistically operate.
- Process as trust substitute — piling on sign-offs instead of building genuine safety.
- Docs theater — writing documents no one reads or that never influence decisions.
- Inclusion as a checkbox — hiring for diversity while excluding those same voices from decisions.

## Maturity model

- Level 1 — Initial: Values is accidental and personality-driven; incidents mean blame; knowledge lives in a few heads.
- Level 2 — Managed: Some teams run postmortems and write docs, but practices are inconsistent and not reinforced by leadership.
- Level 3 — Defined: Blameless learning, ownership models, and writing culture are org-wide norms with clear expectations and tooling.
- Level 4 — Optimizing: Culture is continuously measured and improved; safety is high, learning is fast, and practices evolve with evidence.

## Ideas for discussion

- Where in our organization do people not feel safe to say "I don't know" or "I disagree," and why?
- Do our incident reviews change the system, or just assign fault and move on?
- Are we rewarding heroics that we should be engineering away?
- Which important decisions from the past two years have no written record of their reasoning?
- How evenly is glue work and on-call load distributed across the team?
- Do our stated values match what actually gets people promoted here?

## Key takeaways

- Values are the invisible operating system behind every technical decision; at scale, values must be explicit.
- Psychological safety is foundational; without it, other practices decay.
- Blameless learning converts failure into durable systemic improvement.
- Clear ownership ("you build it, you run it") tightens the quality feedback loop.
- Writing culture scales judgment across time zones and turnover.
- Sustainable pace and inclusion are long-term velocity multipliers, not costs.
