# 12.0 Introduction to Part 12: Project Management

Part 12 is about turning intent into delivered outcomes. Software rarely fails for purely technical reasons; it fails because the right work did not happen in the right order, because dependencies went unmanaged, because risks surfaced too late, and because someone treated an estimate as a promise. Project management is the discipline that guards against those failures — coordinating people, scope, schedule, cost, risk, and quality so that work actually finishes and delivers value. This part treats it not as heavyweight Gantt-chart bureaucracy but as the lightest set of habits that still meets your obligations.

The central choice running through this part is how to deliver: predictive (plan-driven), adaptive (agile), or hybrid. There is no universally correct model — only a fit between method and context. Predictive delivery suits stable, well-understood requirements and hard external constraints; adaptive delivery suits the far more common case where requirements are discovered through building; hybrid delivery wraps an adaptive core inside a predictive governance shell. Getting this choice right, and making it deliberately rather than by habit, is one of the highest-leverage decisions a program makes.

For large teams — and especially for enterprise and government — the stakes are unusually high. With many teams, vendors, and multi-quarter horizons, small coordination failures compound into large, visible overruns. Enterprises must fit delivery to portfolio strategy and budget cycles. Governments add procurement rules, multi-year appropriations, contractor management, and public accountability, where the historical default of big, fixed-scope waterfall contracts has a long record of expensive, public failure. The chapters here show how to satisfy funding cycles and oversight without freezing the delivery that needs room to learn.

## Chapters in this part

- **12.1 Project management** — Covers the fundamentals that apply across predictive, adaptive, and hybrid delivery: managing scope against the schedule–cost–quality triangle, estimating honestly in ranges, mapping dependencies and the critical path, running a living risk register, and keeping stakeholders engaged with transparent status.
- **12.2 Agile** — Goes deep on adaptive delivery as a mindset of values and principles — not a set of ceremonies — covering Scrum, Kanban, and XP as starting points, the non-negotiable role of technical excellence, scaling versus descaling, and how to make agility real inside enterprise and government constraints.

## How these chapters interrelate

The two chapters move from the general to the specific. Chapter 12.1 establishes project management as a whole: the constraints every project lives under, the deliberate choice among predictive, adaptive, and hybrid delivery, and the disciplines — honest estimation, dependency management, early and continuous risk work, engaged stakeholders — that apply no matter which method you choose. Chapter 12.2 then goes deep on the adaptive option, treating Agile as continuous risk reduction through short cycles of working software and real feedback. Read together, they resolve the field's defining tension — predictability versus adaptability — the same way: commit firmly to outcomes and cadence while letting scope flex, and use hybrid governance to satisfy oversight without stalling delivery.

These chapters do not stand alone. Both build on chapter 1.4 (ways of working), which surveys delivery methods broadly, and connect upward to chapter 10.1 (portfolio and program management), which governs the strategy, funding, and coordination above any single project. Agile in particular is powered by the discovery and delivery pipelines of chapters 11.1 and 11.2, and by the flow and queueing insight of chapter 11.3 — empirical throughput forecasts better than heroic estimates, and limiting work in progress shortens lead time. The technical practices that make adaptive delivery sustainable live elsewhere in the guidebook: continuous integration, automated testing, trunk-based development, and clean design. The throughline across all of it: match the method to the work, surface problems while they are still cheap to fix, and prefer the lightest process that meets your real obligations.
